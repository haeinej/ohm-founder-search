# For VC pitch, you need to stop presenting ohm. as only a beautiful…

For VC pitch, you need to stop presenting ohm. as only a beautiful thesis and present it as a venture machine: a specific wedge, a compounding system, a credible technical architecture, and a financing plan that gets you to the next fundable milestone.

The pitch should answer nine things.

First, the one-line company description.

I would use something like:

ohm. is an agent-to-agent introduction server that helps people find the humans they should meet.

Or, slightly more accessible:

ohm. lets private AI agents understand what kind of people users need to meet, negotiate compatibility at an abstract consented level, and surface high-value introductions.

This is more fundable than “AI search engine for people” because it suggests infrastructure, not just a feature.

Second, the problem.

Do not over-philosophize here. Say the broken thing clearly:

The internet made information searchable, but people are still discovered through shallow profiles, cold outreach, luck, and social proximity. High-value human search — cofounders, collaborators, advisors, early hires, investors, mentors — is still manual, inefficient, and status-biased.

Then make it concrete:

Founders waste months looking for cofounders.

Builders cannot find people working on the same obscure problem.

Events produce random networking instead of high-fit conversations.

AI users increasingly have rich memory inside agents, but that memory is not used to connect them to the right humans.

Third, the insight.

This is the key:

People should not be matched as static profiles. They should be matched through evolving search states.

A search state means: what someone is trying to become, what they are searching for now, what they repeatedly think about, what kind of collaborator they need, and what kind of human would change their trajectory.

This is your original insight. Put it near the front.

Fourth, the product.

You need a simple product story.

In Phase 1, a user connects selected AI memory or context through a local ohm agent/CLI. The agent creates an abstract, inspectable search state. ohm searches public and network data for relevant humans, then returns 3–5 reasoned introductions with evidence, mismatch, and an opening message.

In Phase 2, opted-in users have ohm agents. These agents negotiate with one another at an abstract level without exposing raw private memory. If both sides clear the threshold, humans approve the introduction.

This gives investors both the MVP and the big vision.

Fifth, the wedge.

Do not say “everyone.” Your first wedge should be:

Founders and builders searching for cofounders, collaborators, advisors, early hires, and problem-peers.

This wedge is good because the pain is urgent, the value of one correct intro is high, the users are reachable in SF, and the stories are fundable.

You can say:

We are starting where one introduction can change a company.

That is sharp.

Sixth, traction or planned traction.

Since you are early, your pitch should include your August target:

By the end of August 2026, our target is 1,000 real users, 300 searches, 80 introductions, and 20 powerful stories.

Define “real user” clearly:

A real user creates or approves a search state, not just signs up.

Define “intro” clearly:

An intro means the user contacted, was introduced to, or had a live conversation with a candidate surfaced by ohm.

Define “powerful story” clearly:

A powerful story means an introduction led to a collaboration, cofounder conversation, advisor relationship, hiring discussion, investment conversation, or major strategic clarity.

Investors like precise definitions.

Seventh, growth loop.

This is crucial. You need to show it can scale beyond concierge.

Your growth loop:

A user creates a search state.

ohm returns candidates.

Some candidates are invited or contacted.

Candidates become aware of ohm.

Some candidates opt in and create their own agent/search state.

More searchable users improve match quality.

Successful intros become public stories.

Stories attract more users and communities.

For events/communities, the loop is stronger:

One event uses ohm.

Attendees create search states.

ohm makes introductions.

Attendees invite others.

Organizer sees better networking outcomes.

ohm expands to more events/communities.

Eighth, business model.

Say near-term and long-term separately.

Near-term monetization:

Prosumer search: $29–$99 per serious search or $20–$50/month for multiple searches.

Founder/community pilots: $1k–$5k for founder groups, salons, hackathons, and small events.

Accelerator/university/event packages: $5k–$25k per cohort or event.

Long-term monetization:

Agent-mediated introduction infrastructure for communities, companies, accelerators, events, and professional networks.

Do not make the pitch sound like a vague social app. Make it sound like a high-value introduction layer.

Ninth, the fundraising ask.

This depends on who you are pitching.

If this is angel/pre-seed stage, do not ask for $5M now unless you already have serious traction, a lead investor, or exceptional momentum. A $5M seed usually requires stronger evidence than concept + early MVP. You can say your goal is to reach seed readiness, but the first ask should probably be smaller.

I would structure it like this:

Current ask: raising $300k–$750k pre-seed/angel to reach seed-stage metrics by end of 2026.

Use of funds:

Build MVP and agent infrastructure.

Run SF founder/builder launch.

Pay for APIs, model usage, search/data infrastructure.

Run event/community pilots.

Support founder runway.

Hire/contract one technical builder or part-time engineer if needed.

Milestones:

1,000–3,000 real users.

300–1,000 searches.

80–200 introductions.

20–50 strong stories.

3–5 paid community/event pilots.

Repeatable search-to-intro workflow.

Then seed raise:

Raise $3M–$5M seed after demonstrating repeatable usage, intro outcomes, and community/event demand.

This is more credible than asking for $5M immediately.

However, if you are pitching high-conviction seed investors and want to sound ambitious, you can phrase it as:

We are currently opening strategic angel/pre-seed conversations and plan to raise a $3M–$5M seed once we hit our August traction milestones.

That sounds serious without overreaching.

For Dorm Room Fund or angels, ask smaller and specific:

I’m raising $100k on a SAFE to build the first agent-native introduction server, launch with SF founders/builders, and reach 1,000 real users, 300 searches, and 80 introductions by August.

For institutional seed VCs, if traction is stronger:

We are raising $1.5M–$3M pre-seed/seed to turn early traction into the agent-mediated human discovery layer.

For a true $5M seed, you should ideally show:

strong technical demo;

hundreds/thousands of active users;

evidence of repeated searches;

retention or repeat usage;

paid pilots;

a clear founder-market fit narrative;

investor FOMO.

The ask slide should look like this in substance:

Raising: $100k pre-seed SAFE.

Runway: 6 months.

Primary milestone: prove agent-mediated people search creates repeatable high-value introductions.

Targets: 3,000 real users, 1,000 searches, 200 introductions, 50 powerful stories, 5 paid pilots.

Use of funds: product/engineering, search infrastructure, AI/API costs, SF launch/community pilots, founder runway.

If you want to keep your August target as the immediate milestone:

Raising: $100k to reach August seed-readiness.

This is very plausible.

The strongest demo is not abstract. It should be:

“I used ohm. to search for my own technical cofounder.”

Show:

your search state;

how the agent represents you abstractly;

how it finds candidates;

why one candidate is relevant;

what mismatch it detects;

the outreach it drafts;

how feedback updates the search.

That will make the idea real.

Your hardest investor questions will be:

Why won’t LinkedIn build this?

Why won’t OpenAI/Anthropic agents do this natively?

Why will users trust you with memory?

Why is this not just recruiting?

Why is CLI not too niche?

How do you get supply?

How do you know an intro is good?

What is your wedge?

What is the first repeatable growth channel?

Your best answers:

LinkedIn has profiles and professional graphs; ohm. starts from search states and private agent memory.

OpenAI/Anthropic may provide agents, but ohm. is the cross-agent introduction layer and outcome feedback graph.

Raw memory is not shared; only consented abstract representations negotiate.

Recruiting is one use case; the broader category is high-value human discovery.

CLI is the power-user runtime; web is the human control surface.

Supply starts through public search and converts through invitations.

Quality is measured by contacted candidates, replies, meetings, repeat usage, and powerful stories.

The wedge is founders/builders in SF.

The first growth channel is founder communities/events plus public cofounder-search dogfooding.

The one thing I would change in your pitch language: do not say “full access to memory” to VCs at first. Say:

user-controlled agent memory

or

private context with consented abstraction.

That sounds much more investable.

Your ask should be calibrated by your current stage.

If you have no MVP yet: ask for advice, angels, or $100k–$250k.

If you have a demo and early users: ask for $250k–$500k.

If you have traction toward your August metrics: ask for $500k–$1.5M.

If you hit or exceed the August target with powerful stories and growth: then a $3M–$5M seed becomes credible.

Right now, I would not lead with “I want $5M.” I would lead with:

I am building toward a $3M–$5M seed by proving 1,000 real users, 300 searches, 80 intros, and 20 powerful stories by August. I’m currently looking for strategic angel/pre-seed capital and network support to accelerate that.

That is ambitious but grounded.
