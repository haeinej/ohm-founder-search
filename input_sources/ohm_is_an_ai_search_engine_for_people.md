# ohm is an AI search engine for people.

ohm is an AI search engine for people.

Tell us who you’re looking for. We find them.

0. First Principle

The most important opportunities in a person’s life often arrive through another human being.

A cofounder can change the trajectory of a company. A mentor can compress years of uncertainty into one conversation. A romantic partner can reshape one’s understanding of freedom, intimacy, and home. A collaborator can unlock an idea that was impossible to build alone.

Yet the internet has become extraordinarily good at helping us find information and remarkably poor at helping us find the right people.

Search engines organize knowledge. Social platforms organize attention. Dating apps organize desire. Professional networks organize credentials. But none of them are designed to answer a more existential and practical question:

Who is the person I am looking for, even if I do not know their name?

The right person is rarely defined by demographics, job title, location, or surface-level interests. They are defined by trajectory, values, pace, taste, emotional rhythm, intellectual obsession, and the kind of future they are trying to build.

The first principle behind ohm is that human connection should not begin from visibility, performance, or proximity alone. It should begin from resonance: the recognition that another person is carrying a question, intensity, or direction that meaningfully intersects with one’s own.

ohm is built on the belief that AI should not only answer human questions. It should help humans find the other humans with whom those questions become alive.

1. Problem

People do not lack access to other people. They lack access to the right people.

We live inside an abundance of weak connection: followers, matches, contacts, comments, group chats, events, feeds, and recommendations. But most of these systems are optimized for visibility, engagement, or categorical filtering, not for profound compatibility or transformative encounter.

This creates several failures.

First, existing platforms reduce people into shallow proxies. LinkedIn represents credentials. Dating apps represent appearance and short prompts. Social media represents performance. Communities represent topical affiliation. None of these surfaces reliably capture how someone thinks, what they are becoming, how they make decisions, what kind of emotional or intellectual rhythm they carry, or whether conversation with them would genuinely expand another person’s life.

Second, the burden of search remains on the individual. Someone looking for a technical cofounder, a creative collaborator, a mentor, or a romantic partner must manually browse platforms, interpret fragmented signals, send cold messages, and hope that the right person happens to be visible. The current internet assumes that if the right person exists, the user can somehow find them. In practice, this is false.

Third, most matching systems are profile-first rather than intent-first. They begin by asking, “Who are you?” instead of “What kind of person are you searching for right now?” But human compatibility is contextual. The version of oneself seeking a cofounder is different from the version seeking a romantic partner, a mentor, a collaborator, or an intellectual peer. A single static profile cannot represent the complexity of human search.

Fourth, AI currently deepens the paradox. LLMs can answer almost any question, generate options, and simulate advice. But some problems are not solved by answers alone. They are solved through conversation, trust, contradiction, lived experience, and the strange intelligence of another human being. In those moments, the better question is not “What should ChatGPT tell me?” but “Who should I be talking to?”

The result is a major unsolved gap: people have powerful tools to find information, but weak tools to find the humans who could change their lives.

2. Hypothesis

The core hypothesis of ohm is that the most valuable human matches can be discovered by modeling people not as profiles, but as evolving search states.

A person is not simply a list of traits. A person is a history, a set of recurring questions, a current quest, a rhythm of attention, a pattern of attraction and avoidance, and a direction of becoming.

If an AI agent can understand a user’s private context deeply enough, it can generate a richer and more accurate representation of whom that person is searching for. This representation can then be matched against other people’s public or permissioned signals to surface unusually meaningful human connections.

The key hypothesis is not that AI can replace human connection. It is the opposite.

ohm assumes that human beings remain irreplaceable. The role of AI is not to become the friend, lover, cofounder, or mentor. The role of AI is to help find them.

This leads to a second hypothesis: the best matching experience may not begin with visible browsing between humans, but with invisible negotiation between agents.

Each user can have a private agent that understands their context: writings, notes, conversations, projects, saved ideas, preferences, dealbreakers, and current goals. The agent does not expose raw private data. Instead, it compresses relevant context into consented, purpose-specific representations.

For example, a user looking for a technical cofounder might be represented differently from the same user looking for a romantic partner. The cofounder-search representation might emphasize technical complementarity, philosophical alignment, pace, ambition, and trust. The romantic-search representation might emphasize freedom, emotional depth, humor, independence, and life rhythm.

The deeper hypothesis is that the future of matching is not static profiles. It is context-specific, agent-mediated human search.

3. Approach / Solution

ohm is an AI-native human search engine that helps people find the rare individuals they are looking for but cannot easily describe through conventional filters.

The product begins with a simple question:

What kind of person would change your life if you found them?

From there, ohm builds a purpose-specific search representation. The user may be searching for a technical cofounder, a creative collaborator, a mentor, an intellectual peer, or eventually a romantic partner. Instead of asking the user to manually construct a profile, ohm uses AI to help articulate the deeper shape of the search.

The system has four core layers.

The first layer is private context. Users may provide selected sources such as writing, notes, ChatGPT conversations, essays, project descriptions, saved links, or other personal knowledge. This data remains private and permissioned. The purpose is not to expose the user, but to help the agent understand them with more nuance.

The second layer is self-modeling. The agent extracts patterns from the user’s context: recurring questions, values, intellectual taste, ambition level, collaboration style, emotional needs, aesthetic preferences, communication patterns, dealbreakers, and current direction. This becomes a private model of the user’s search-relevant self.

The third layer is intent-specific representation. When the user declares a search goal, the agent creates a temporary representation for that specific context. The same person may have different representations depending on whether they are searching for a cofounder, mentor, collaborator, or partner. This avoids the flattening effect of universal profiles.

The fourth layer is agentic matching. The system compares the user’s search representation with either public human signals or permissioned representations from other users. In the earliest version, ohm can search across public data such as personal websites, essays, GitHub, Substack, startup profiles, speaker pages, communities, or other publicly available professional and intellectual traces. Over time, as more users opt in, agents can communicate with one another directly, exchanging limited, consented summaries to determine whether a human introduction is worth making.

The output is not a feed of random people. The output is a small number of highly reasoned introductions.

For each candidate, ohm explains why the person may matter, what evidence supports the match, what potential mismatch exists, and how to start the conversation. The user receives not only a name, but a reason and a possible opening.

In the earliest MVP, the product can be narrow and concrete: help founders find unusually aligned cofounders, collaborators, and thought partners. This use case is high-intent, publicly verifiable, and easier to validate than dating. If ohm can help a founder find one person they would not have discovered otherwise, the product has already delivered meaningful value.

Over time, the same infrastructure can expand into other forms of human search: mentors, creative collaborators, research partners, intellectual peers, community members, and eventually romantic relationships.

4. Value Proposition

ohm helps people find the humans they are actually searching for, not just the humans who are easiest to filter, swipe, or follow.

For founders, ohm can surface technical cofounders, advisors, early teammates, or collaborators whose alignment is not visible from credentials alone. It can identify people who share the underlying question behind a company, not merely the skill set required to execute it.

For creatives and researchers, ohm can find intellectual peers who are working around similar questions from different angles. This can create new collaborations, essays, projects, companies, and communities.

For individuals, ohm can eventually help locate people who match deeper emotional and existential criteria: people who respect freedom, share a wavelength of humor and depth, and expand rather than reduce one’s sense of self.

The value is not “more matches.” The value is fewer, better, more consequential introductions.

Existing platforms optimize for volume, engagement, and continuous browsing. ohm optimizes for precision, depth, and life relevance.

A successful ohm match should feel less like content recommendation and more like recognition:

This is someone I did not know how to find, but somehow needed to meet.

The defensibility of ohm comes from its ability to build richer self-representations over time. As users interact with the system, refine their searches, accept or reject introductions, and provide feedback, their agents become better at understanding what resonance means for them. This creates a proprietary layer of human preference and relational intelligence that generic social platforms do not possess.

The immediate value proposition is practical: find a cofounder, collaborator, mentor, or thought partner.

The long-term value proposition is existential: help people locate the rare human relationships that alter the direction of their lives.

5. North Star

The North Star of ohm is not daily active users, swipe volume, or time spent in app.

The North Star is the number of life-altering introductions created.

A life-altering introduction is a connection that leads to a meaningful outcome: a company formed, a collaboration started, a mentor relationship developed, a major decision clarified, a creative project launched, a person feeling deeply understood, or eventually a lasting romantic partnership.

The core product question is:

Did ohm help someone meet a person they would not have found otherwise, and did that person change what became possible?

In the short term, this can be measured through high-signal proxy metrics: introductions accepted, reply rates, conversation quality ratings, second conversations, collaborations started, and testimonials from users who felt the match was unusually precise.

In the long term, ohm should become the infrastructure for human discovery across domains. Search engines help users find information. Recommendation systems help users find content. ohm should help users find people.

The ultimate vision is that when someone is searching for a cofounder, a mentor, a collaborator, a thinking partner, or a partner in life, they do not begin by scrolling. They begin by asking ohm:

Who should I meet?

6. Why I Am Doing This

I am building ohm because I have repeatedly felt that the most important questions in my life could not be resolved by information alone.

I could ask ChatGPT for answers. I could read essays, search the internet, browse profiles, attend events, and message strangers. But what I was really looking for was not more information. I was looking for the right human being: someone who could think with me, challenge me, understand the invisible part of what I was building, and push me beyond the limits of my own mind.

As a founder, I have asked myself whether I could find a technical cofounder who not only has the skill to build, but also shares the philosophical weight of the problem. I do not simply need an engineer. I need someone who understands why human resonance matters, why social platforms feel broken, why AI should not replace human connection, and why the right collaborator can change the trajectory of a company.

As a person, I have also wondered whether I could find a future partner who respects my freedom while sharing the same wavelength of depth, humor, intensity, and tenderness. Existing dating apps are not built to search for that. They are built to filter, display, and accelerate judgment. They cannot easily represent the complexity of wanting both independence and intimacy, ambition and softness, seriousness and play.

These are not separate problems. They are versions of the same deeper problem.

We do not know how to find the people who belong in the next chapter of our lives.

I am building ohm because I believe AI can be used differently. Instead of making people more isolated inside synthetic conversations, AI can become a bridge back to human beings. It can help us understand ourselves more clearly, articulate whom we are truly searching for, and discover people who would otherwise remain invisible.

The most powerful technology for human potential was never artificial intelligence alone. It was always another human being.

ohm exists to find that human.
